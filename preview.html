Beginner-first structured products guide

Structured products, explained without the fog.

A structured product is an investment with pre-agreed rules. Those rules decide when you receive income, when you get capital back, when the note ends early, and what market event can cause a loss.

Bank-issued note + underlying market + payoff rules = structured product

The beginner mistake is to ask, "What is the coupon?" first. The right first question is: what has to happen for me to get paid, and what has to happen for me to lose money?

The building blocks

Learn these before learning product names. Most confusion in structured products comes from mixing up barriers, protection, coupon conditions and issuer risk.

Issuer

The bank or vehicle that issues the note. You rely on its ability to pay.

Underlying

The index, stock, fund, basket, interest rate or credit index linked to the payoff.

Initial level

The starting reference level. Future performance is usually measured against it.

Maturity

The final date when the payoff is calculated, unless the note redeems early.

Observation date

A scheduled check date. Autocalls and coupons are often tested quarterly or annually.

Coupon barrier

The level the underlying must meet for income to be paid.

Autocall barrier

The level that triggers early redemption, usually repaying capital plus any due coupon.

Capital barrier

The level below which capital can be reduced at maturity.

Worst-of basket

The worst-performing asset controls the result. This increases coupon but increases risk.

Participation

The percentage of positive market performance you receive.

Cap

The maximum upside you can receive, even if the underlying rises more.

Secondary market

The possible sale price before maturity. It can be below capital protection or issue price.

The four questions to ask every time

1. What must happen for income to be paid?
2. What must happen for capital to be returned?
3. What event causes capital loss?
4. Who must remain solvent for the note to pay?

Beginner trap

A 70% capital barrier does not mean the maximum loss is 30%. If the final level is 50%, many notes may repay around 50% of capital. The barrier is a trigger, not a loss cap.

Capital protected participation note

Exposure to upside performance with capital protection at maturity, subject to issuer credit risk.

Participation
Plain English: If the underlying rises, you participate in some or all of the gain. If it falls, the note aims to repay capital at maturity.
Do not oversell it: capital protection is not a magic shield. It usually applies only at maturity and only if the issuer can pay.

Credit-linked note

A note that pays yield because the investor accepts credit-event risk on a reference entity or credit index.

Credit
Plain English: You receive a coupon for taking a defined credit risk. If no credit event occurs, the note usually repays as expected.
Two risks, not one: you have issuer risk on the note issuer and reference-entity risk on the company or index named in the CLN.
EventPossible result
No credit eventCoupon paid and capital repaid according to terms.
Reference entity credit eventCapital may be reduced to recovery value or by tranche rules.
Issuer defaultThe note may fail even if the reference entity is fine.

Range accrual callable CLN

Combines credit risk with an interest-rate range accrual mechanism. Coupon depends on how many days the reference rate stays inside the range.

Credit + rates

Coupon logic: coupon = headline rate x days in range / total days. If the rate spends only half the period in range, the coupon is roughly half the headline coupon, before considering other terms.

Callable risk: if the structure becomes attractive for the issuer to redeem, the issuer may call it, leaving the investor to reinvest in a new market environment.

Certificates of deposit

Cash-like instruments issued by banks, typically used for short-term yield and currency cash management.

Cash
Cleaner wording: CDs have no equity-market exposure if held to maturity, but they still carry issuer/bank credit risk, liquidity risk and currency risk.

Actively Managed Certificate

A certificate that packages a managed strategy into one investable security with an ISIN.

Platform

Use AMCs to explain access and operational efficiency, not just return. The key advantage is that a strategy can be rebalanced inside one certificate rather than forcing each investor to process every underlying trade.

Safer wording: avoid saying "no issuer risk" too broadly. Say "designed to reduce issuer balance-sheet risk through the chosen structure, subject to documentation, custody, service-provider and asset-level risk."

The risks that must be visible

The best beginner explainer does not hide the risk section at the bottom. Put the risk beside each benefit.

Issuer credit risk

Capital protection and coupon promises depend on the issuer's ability to pay. A rating is not a guarantee.

Market and barrier risk

If the underlying finishes below a barrier or strike, capital can be reduced, sometimes substantially.

Worst-of risk

The weakest asset can determine the outcome. A diversified-looking basket may behave like a single bad stock.

Liquidity and mark-to-market risk

Before maturity, the secondary-market value may be below the protected amount or issue price.

Reinvestment risk

Autocall can repay capital early, often when the structure has worked well, forcing reinvestment at new terms.

Complexity risk

Small wording differences in the term sheet can change the payoff. Always read the final terms.

Glossary

Use this as a beginner safety net. The more technical the product, the more visible the glossary should be.

Autocall

Automatic early redemption if a condition is met on an observation date.

Barrier

A trigger level used to decide coupon payment, autocall or capital loss.

Capital protection

Defined repayment protection at maturity, subject to issuer risk and final terms.

Coupon

The income paid by the note, fixed or conditional depending on the structure.

Memory coupon

A missed coupon that can be paid later if future conditions are met.

Worst-of

A basket payoff based on the worst-performing underlying.

Participation

The percentage of positive performance passed to the investor.

Cap

The maximum return or upside participation allowed by the product.

Strike

The reference level used to calculate payoff or downside exposure.

Issuer risk

The risk that the note issuer cannot meet its payment obligations.

Credit event

A defined event such as bankruptcy, failure to pay or restructuring in a CLN.

Mark-to-market

The estimated value before maturity, which can be below capital protection.

Advanced: how the bank manufactures the payoff

This belongs after the beginner journey, not before it. Use it for advisers who want the engineering logic.

The coupon is funded by option economics, issuer funding, volatility, correlation and the investor accepting downside risk. Higher coupons usually mean a less forgiving structure, riskier underlyings, higher volatility or more issuer/reference risk.
Part of the investment economics is used to support capital repayment at maturity. The remaining option budget buys market exposure. That is why upside may be capped or participation may be below, equal to or above 100% depending on rates, volatility, dividends, tenor and issuer terms.
The investor is effectively exposed to the weakest asset in the basket. The probability that at least one asset performs badly is higher than the probability that a single broad index performs badly, so the coupon can be higher.
